## Summary

This exploratory laboratory study provides further preclinical evidence supporting the role of transient receptor potential melastatin 3 (TRPM3) ion channel dysfunction in ME/CFS. By demonstrating that impaired TRPM3 channels directly affect calcium transport into the mitochondria of natural killer cells, the research strengthens our understanding of cellular energy deficits in the disease. However, because the study was performed ex vivo on a very small cohort of ten patients, it serves as a preliminary pathway validation rather than an immediate diagnostic test or clinical treatment. For patients, this work clarifies how calcium channel dysfunction translates into the mitochondrial energy exhaustion commonly associated with post-exertional malaise, while offering long-term directions for drug development.

## What was researched?

The study investigated the downstream impact of transient receptor potential melastatin 3 (TRPM3) ion channel dysfunction on mitochondrial calcium (Ca2+) mobilisation in the natural killer (NK) cells of individuals with ME/CFS.

## Why was it researched?

Previous research has linked ME/CFS to impaired TRPM3 ion channel function and reduced cytosolic calcium mobilisation in NK cells, which contributes to impaired cellular cytotoxicity. This study aimed to explore how these alterations further cascade downstream to affect mitochondrial calcium buffering, which is essential for cell bioenergetics and survival.

## How was it researched?

Fluorescence live-cell imaging was conducted on NK cells isolated from 10 patients with ME/CFS (defined by the Canadian Consensus Criteria) and 10 healthy controls. Cytoplasmic and mitochondrial Ca2+ entry were monitored using the specialized fluorescent indicators Fluo-8 AM and Rhod-2 AM, respectively. The investigators modulated TRPM3 channel activity ex vivo by applying the agonist pregnenolone sulfate 💊 to activate the channel and ononetin to inhibit it.

## What has been found?

The researchers found that cytosolic Ca2+ influx amplitude and slope were significantly reduced in ME/CFS NK cells compared to healthy controls, showing a faster decay time. Conversely, baseline Ca2+ influx into the mitochondria was significantly higher in ME/CFS cells. Upon stimulation with the TRPM3 agonist, healthy control cells showed robust cytosolic and mitochondrial calcium increases, whereas ME/CFS cells demonstrated significantly reduced TRPM3-dependent calcium mobilisation in both compartments. This highlights a clear disruption in the coordination of calcium transport between the cytoplasm and the mitochondria in ME/CFS.

## Discussion

The study's primary strength is the precise use of real-time fluorescence imaging to track calcium flow into specific cellular compartments. A key limitation is the very small sample size of only ten patients and ten controls, which necessitates verification in much larger cohorts. Furthermore, while these findings provide crucial mechanistic details about NK cell dysfunction, the extent to which these mitochondrial calcium deficits occur in other cell types remains to be determined.

## Conclusion & Future Work

The authors concluded that impaired TRPM3-mediated cytosolic calcium influx significantly disrupts calcium entry into the mitochondria of NK cells in ME/CFS patients. This dual cytoplasmic and mitochondrial signaling deficit likely contributes to the compromised immune function and energy exhaustion seen in the disease. Future studies should focus on identifying drugs that can target this pathway to restore calcium homeostasis.
