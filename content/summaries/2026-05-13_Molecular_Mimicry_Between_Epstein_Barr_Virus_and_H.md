## Summary

This computational study offers a plausible explanation for how common viral infections like EBV and HHV-6 might trigger or worsen neurological symptoms in patients with post-viral conditions, including ME/CFS and multiple sclerosis (MS). By identifying 91 identical protein segments shared between these viruses and vital brain proteins, the findings suggest that a patient's immune response to a viral flare-up could mistakenly attack central nervous system tissues. For patients and caregivers, this mapping helps validate their neurological symptoms as biochemically credible post-viral reactions rather than psychosomatic complaints. Ultimately, this work provides specific, high-priority molecular targets that could guide the future development of diagnostic autoantibody tests and highly targeted neuro-immunotherapies.

## What was researched?

This in silico study investigated the sequence similarities and molecular mimicry between Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) and human herpesvirus-6 (HHV-6) antigens and various central nervous system (CNS) autoantigens. Specifically, the researchers analyzed the occurrence of shared pentapeptides and evaluated their potential to activate T and B cells.

## Why was it researched?

Both EBV and HHV-6 are frequently linked to neuroimmune and neuropsychiatric conditions like multiple sclerosis (MS) and myalgic encephalomyelitis/chronic fatigue syndrome (ME/CFS). It is hypothesized that these viruses trigger or drive these disorders by inducing autoimmune reactions against self-proteins in the brain through molecular mimicry.

## How was it researched?

The researchers systematically compared 32 immunogenic viral antigens from EBV and HHV-6 against 10 key CNS autoantigens. Using protein sequences retrieved from the UniProt database, they performed sequence alignments to identify identical shared pentapeptides. Structural homologies were then modeled using AlphaFold and PyMOL, and the immunogenicity of these shared regions for human T and B cells was assessed using the Immune Epitope Database (IEDB).

## What has been found?

The analysis identified a total of 91 mimicry pentapeptides between the herpesvirus antigens and CNS proteins. Synapsin (SYN)1 exhibited the most significant molecular mimicry, sharing multiple pentapeptides with several EBV and HHV-6 antigens (such as EBNA1, EBNA6, LMP1, and EA-D). Myelin-associated proteins, including myelin basic protein (MBP), myelin-associated glycoprotein (MAG), and myelin-oligodendrocyte glycoprotein (MOG), also contained shared immunogenic pentapeptides. Furthermore, most of the analyzed viral proteins displayed structurally resembling motifs capable of triggering cross-reactive T- and B-cell immunogenicity.

## Discussion

A major limitation of this research is its strictly computational, in silico nature, meaning the predicted molecular mimicry and immune cross-reactivity have not yet been validated in living biological models or clinical samples. Nevertheless, these computational predictions provide a high-resolution blueprint that highlights specific, highly suspect viral-host protein interfaces for future experimental investigations.

## Conclusion & Future Work

The study concludes that substantial molecular mimicry exists between common neurotropic herpesviruses and key brain proteins, supporting a potential autoimmune mechanism in post-viral neurological illnesses. The authors suggest that future wet-lab validation using peptide-based assays and animal models is necessary to confirm these cross-reactive immune pathways.
