## Summary

This study provides clear biomarker-based evidence linking cognitive symptoms, such as brain fog and verbal memory issues, directly to ongoing blood vessel inflammation and endothelial activation. For patients and caregivers, this shifts the understanding of neuropsychiatric Long COVID away from purely psychological explanations and points to testable, biological dysfunction in the circulatory system. Although the vascular markers appeared to normalize in the very late-stage cohort (nearly three years post-infection), the findings during the one-year mark highlight a critical window where endothelial-targeted treatments or anti-inflammatory interventions might prevent long-term cognitive damage.

## What was researched?

This study investigated whether vascular inflammation and endothelial dysfunction are present in patients experiencing neuropsychiatric Long COVID. It also evaluated how specific blood vessel inflammatory markers relate to cognitive and mental health symptoms.

## Why was it researched?

Neuropsychiatric symptoms, such as cognitive impairment and mood changes, are among the most debilitating aspects of Long COVID, yet their underlying biological causes remain poorly understood. While acute COVID-19 is known to cause severe vascular damage, it is unclear if persistent, low-grade blood vessel inflammation drives chronic post-viral neurological symptoms.

## How was it researched?

This cross-sectional, case-control study analyzed cohorts from the Yale COVID Mind Study, Yale IMPACT Study, and Johns Hopkins University Long COVID Study. Researchers compared 50 participants with neuropsychiatric Long COVID (median 368 days post-infection) to 28 individuals with acute COVID-19 and 29 recovered controls. An independent late-stage cohort of 114 Long COVID patients (median 1,065 days post-infection) and 31 recovered controls was also evaluated. The team measured 14 plasma biomarkers of vascular inflammation and administered comprehensive neuropsychiatric and cognitive assessments to non-hospitalized participants.

## What has been found?

Participants with neuropsychiatric Long COVID demonstrated significantly elevated markers of endothelial, leukocyte, and platelet adhesion (including sL-selectin, ADAMTS13, sP-selectin, and sICAM-1) compared to recovered controls, whereas standard clotting markers did not differ. Certain biomarkers, such as fetuin, sL-selectin, and α-2 macroglobulin, were even higher in Long COVID patients than in those with acute infection. Furthermore, higher sP-selectin levels correlated with poorer verbal fluency and learning, while lower α1-acid glycoprotein levels strongly associated with cognitive decline, depression, and anxiety. Interestingly, these differences were absent in the late-stage cohort, suggesting vascular inflammation may resolve or change as the disease progresses past three years.

## Discussion

The study's strengths include its robust multi-cohort design and the direct linking of blood-based endothelial markers to standardized neurocognitive scores. A key limitation is the cross-sectional nature, which prevents drawing causal conclusions about vascular damage and brain fog. Additionally, the lack of biomarker differences in the late-stage cohort indicates that vascular-driven pathophysiology might be time-dependent or undergo a transition in chronic phases.

## Conclusion & Future Work

Persistent endothelial activation and platelet-leukocyte adhesion are key features of neuropsychiatric Long COVID up to a year post-infection and directly correlate with cognitive dysfunction. These results suggest that targeting endothelial health and vascular inflammation could be a promising therapeutic avenue for patients. Future longitudinal research is needed to track these biomarkers over longer intervals and evaluate vascular-directed therapies.
