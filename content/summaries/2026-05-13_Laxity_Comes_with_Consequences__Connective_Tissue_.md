## Summary

For patients and caregivers, this paper offers a valuable explanatory model for why joint hypermobility, craniocervical instability, and POTS are so frequently coupled with ME/CFS. It suggests that these symptoms are not merely random comorbidities but are driven by a shared, self-sustaining loop of oxidative stress, mast cell activation, and impaired collagen repair. While these insights do not immediately yield a new treatment, they provide a strong scientific rationale for treating underlying comorbidities like mast cell activation early, and help guide future research toward targeted therapeutics that address tissue oxygenation and metabolic recovery.

## What was researched?

This concept paper explores the bidirectional relationship between connective tissue disorders and ME/CFS. Specifically, it investigates how ME/CFS-associated pathophysiological processes might degrade and weaken connective tissues, and how preexisting ligament laxity can predispose individuals to ME/CFS.

## Why was it researched?

Many ME/CFS patients experience symptoms of connective tissue laxity, such as joint hypermobility and craniocervical instability, which are often comorbid with postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome (POTS). Understanding the molecular and cellular mechanisms behind this overlap could clarify why these conditions co-occur and how they contribute to disease chronification.

## How was it researched?

The author performed a comprehensive literature review and developed a mechanistic, systems-based hypothesis. This conceptual framework integrates findings on muscle biopsies, vascular regulation, mast cell activation, and oxygen-sensing cellular pathways to explain connective tissue degradation and impaired remodeling in ME/CFS.

## What has been found?

The author proposes that mast cell overactivity and chronic inflammation release enzymes (tryptase and chymase) that activate collagen-degrading metalloproteinases. Simultaneously, oxidative stress (ROS) and localized hypoxia inhibit prolyl hydroxylases (PHDs), which impairs the stability of newly formed collagen and prevents the degradation of hypoxia-inducible factor 1-alpha (HIF-1α). Chronically elevated HIF-1α alters connective tissue composition unfavorably, while a deficiency in HIF-2α prevents proper capillary maturation, leading to the characteristic basement membrane thickening and reduced capillary density seen in patient muscle biopsies. Finally, because the neck muscles are also affected by ME/CFS-associated metabolic dysfunction, they fail to compensate for lax ligaments, thereby worsening craniocervical instability.

## Discussion

A primary strength of this paper is that it provides a cohesive molecular model connecting hypermobility, vascular leakage, and metabolic dysfunction in ME/CFS. However, a major limitation is that this is a theoretical concept paper, and the proposed mechanisms—such as the specific imbalance between HIF-1α and HIF-2α—require direct verification in clinical cohorts.

## Conclusion & Future Work

The paper concludes that a vicious, bidirectional cycle exists where underlying connective tissue disorders predispose individuals to orthostatic stress and ME/CFS, while ME/CFS pathomechanisms further degrade connective tissues. Future research should focus on direct quantification of HIF-1α/2α and PHD activity in skeletal muscle and connective tissues of ME/CFS patients.
