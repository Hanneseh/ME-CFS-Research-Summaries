## Summary

This study provides crucial biomarker association evidence linking chest pain in ME/CFS to objective cardiovascular and microvascular dysfunction rather than psychological causes. By showing that 86% of tested patients had coronary vasospasms or microvascular dysfunction, it suggests a concrete, testable mechanism (endotheliopathy) for cardiac symptoms in patients. For patients and caregivers, this shifts the paradigm of chest pain in ME/CFS toward recognized medical conditions like vasospastic angina or coronary microvascular disease, which can be managed with standard cardiological treatments. However, because this is an observational pilot cohort without a control group, these findings serve as a strong signal rather than a definitive diagnostic standard.

## What was researched?

This study investigated the prevalence and characteristics of coronary vasomotor disorders, such as coronary vasospasm and microvascular dysfunction, in patients with ME/CFS who experience chest pain. It specifically analyzed the underlying cardiovascular physiology in patients who showed a primary peripheral exercise limitation during invasive cardiopulmonary exercise testing (iCPET).

## Why was it researched?

Chest pain is an increasingly recognized symptom in ME/CFS, but the exact cardiovascular mechanisms behind it remain poorly understood. While iCPET has previously shown that ME/CFS patients suffer from impaired systemic oxygen extraction, this study aimed to determine if similar circulatory and microvascular abnormalities exist within the heart's own blood vessels.

## How was it researched?

Researchers evaluated 25 ME/CFS patients (80% female, mean age 53.44 years) who exhibited a primary peripheral exercise limitation on iCPET. Of these, 22 underwent invasive coronary function testing (CFT), which included acetylcholine provocation for vasoreactivity, guidewire-based coronary flow reserve (CFR), and index of microcirculatory resistance (IMR). The remaining three patients underwent non-invasive cardiac positron emission tomography (PET) CT.

## What has been found?

None of the patients had obstructive atherosclerotic coronary artery disease, but a striking 86% (19 out of 22) of those undergoing invasive testing were diagnosed with a coronary vasomotor disorder. Specifically, coronary vasospasm was found in 14 patients (63.6%), coronary microvascular dysfunction (CMD) in 4 patients (18.2%), and endothelial dysfunction in 4 patients (18.2%). Additionally, all three patients evaluated via PET CT showed evidence of coronary microvascular disease.

## Discussion

The findings show a high incidence of coronary vasomotor disorders in ME/CFS, representing the first description of such abnormalities in this cohort. The authors discuss that these cardiac microvascular impairments might share mechanisms with systemic endotheliopathy, potentially driven by autonomic dysregulation, chronic inflammation, or immune dysfunction. However, the study is limited by its small sample size and lack of a healthy control group.

## Conclusion & Future Work

The authors conclude that coronary vasomotor disorders are common in ME/CFS patients with peripheral exercise limitations and should be clinically considered when these patients present with chest pain. They call for larger studies to clarify the pathobiology of combined systemic and coronary circulatory dysfunction to help identify targeted therapies.
