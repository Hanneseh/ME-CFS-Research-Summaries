## Summary

This paper provides patients and caregivers with a vital biological validation of their experience, especially regarding post-exertional malaise (PEM). By establishing that biological abnormalities are state-dependent, the review scientifically explains why routine resting lab results are often normal while physical challenge causes severe symptoms. This model shifts the therapeutic paradigm away from harmful rehabilitative protocols like graded exercise and guides the medical field toward dynamic diagnostic testing. Ultimately, it maps out concrete biological pathways to target with emerging treatments, offering a promising roadmap for future clinical trials.

## What was researched?

This review paper investigated the historical context and systems biology of myalgic encephalomyelitis/chronic fatigue syndrome (ME/CFS) and related post-infectious syndromes. The authors aimed to synthesize modern biomedical insights into a unified mechanistic model of chronic post-infectious diseases.

## Why was it researched?

Historically, ME/CFS has suffered from diagnostic ambiguity and the dominance of psychosocial and rehabilitative clinical models, such as graded exercise therapy. The rapid expansion of systems biology research accelerated by Long COVID created an opportunity to reframe ME/CFS within a robust, multi-systemic biological framework.

## How was it researched?

The authors performed a comprehensive review and conceptual analysis of literature spanning early post-infectious syndrome observations to contemporary systems biology. They examined key physiological domains including immune regulation, mitochondrial metabolism, and microvascular function, and compiled a repository of candidate therapeutic interventions.

## What has been found?

The review reframes ME/CFS as a disorder of impaired adaptive capacity within post-infectious biology, highlighting that pathology is state-dependent and primarily revealed under physiological stress rather than at rest. The authors linked persistent immune dysregulation, metabolic reprogramming, endothelial dysfunction, abnormal coagulation, extracellular vesicle signaling, and viral protein-associated immune activation. To assist future clinical trials, they compiled a comprehensive chart of potential therapeutic candidates with reported clinical signals, including low-dose naltrexone 💊 and CoQ10 💊.

## Discussion

A major point of discussion is that studying ME/CFS patients solely at rest misses critical pathological signals, which explains why static clinical measurements often appear normal. The authors emphasize that the historical persistence of psychosocial frameworks hindered progress and call for a shift to dynamic, challenge-based investigative approaches.

## Conclusion & Future Work

The authors conclude that ME/CFS must be understood as a dynamic biological condition characterized by an inability to maintain physiological homeostasis under challenge. They recommend that future research prioritize patient stratification and trial designs that utilize stress-provocation testing to assess biological response.
