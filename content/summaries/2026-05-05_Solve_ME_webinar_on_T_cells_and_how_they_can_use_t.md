## Summary

This project represents a major step toward clinical precision medicine by aiming to decode the exact pathogens or self-proteins driving chronic illness. For patients, this could eventually replace trial-and-error treatments with highly personalized therapies, such as matching patients who show herpesvirus-focused T-cell activity to targeted antivirals. As a key institutional milestone, this Solve M.E. Catalyst Award webinar highlights the field's rapid momentum toward objective diagnostic profiling and patient stratification.

## What was researched?

This research initiative investigated the specific target antigens—either persistent pathogens or self-proteins—recognized by dysfunctional and exhausted T cells in patients with ME/CFS and Long COVID.

## Why was it researched?

The study was prompted by findings of chronic T-cell exhaustion and unique immune activation patterns in patients, indicating the immune system is actively fighting an ongoing threat. Identifying these targets is crucial to resolve whether these conditions are driven by hidden viral reservoirs or autoimmune cross-reactivity.

## How was it researched?

The researchers utilize single-cell immune profiling and T-cell receptor (TCR) sequencing on patient blood samples. They screen patient TCRs against comprehensive libraries of human leukocyte antigen (HLA)–displayed microbial protein fragments (representing pathogens like SARS-CoV-2 and Borrelia burgdorferi) and human self-proteins to identify precise binders.

## What has been found?

The researchers previously identified distinct T-cell receptor clusters and exhausted CD8+ T cells unique to ME/CFS and Long COVID cohorts. Under this newly funded Catalyst project, the team is actively mapping these TCRs to their corresponding microbial or autoimmune antigens. This approach aims to classify patients into distinct pathophysiological subgroups based on their unique T-cell patterns.

## Discussion

A major challenge is the extreme diversity of human leukocyte antigens (HLAs) and TCR repertoires across different individuals, which makes large-scale standardization difficult. Additionally, distinguishing between true causative triggers and bystander immune activation requires highly controlled matching and validation.

## Conclusion & Future Work

Mapping the exact targets of dysfunctional T cells could provide a definitive diagnostic biomarker system for ME/CFS and Long COVID. These findings will help transition the field from broad symptom management to personalized, targeted therapies.
