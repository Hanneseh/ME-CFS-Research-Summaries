## Summary

This study provides valuable biomarker association evidence that links G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) autoantibodies with objective autonomic and cognitive features in patients with ME/CFS and post-COVID condition (PCC). While these results reinforce the biological reality of both disorders and point to autonomic dysfunction, they do not yet confirm direct causality due to the lack of functional activity assays. For patients and caregivers, this work highlights the potential of using autoantibody testing for patient stratification or subtyping in research settings. However, it does not immediately translate into a validated diagnostic test or change current therapeutic options. Ultimately, the correlation with clinical symptoms keeps the door open for future trials exploring immunotherapies that target these autoantibodies.

## What was researched?

This study investigated the association between autoantibodies targeting G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs)—specifically alpha-1, beta-1, beta-2 adrenergic, and M1–M4 muscarinic receptors—and physiological markers of autonomic dysfunction and cognitive impairment in patients with post-COVID condition (PCC) and Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (ME/CFS).

## Why was it researched?

Both PCC and ME/CFS are chronic conditions that present with overlapping symptoms like severe fatigue, autonomic imbalance, and cognitive deficits, with a suspected autoimmune basis. This research aimed to clarify if specific G-protein-coupled receptor autoantibodies drive these clinical symptoms, potentially serving as biological markers to help distinguish and understand both disorders.

## How was it researched?

The cross-sectional study included 96 patients with PCC, 59 patients with ME/CFS, and 36 healthy controls. Plasma autoantibody titers against seven GPCRs were measured using enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) testing. Autonomic nervous system function was comprehensively assessed using COMPASS-31, Sudoscan, and clinical hemodynamic testing, while cognitive performance was measured across multiple neurological domains.

## What has been found?

ME/CFS patients demonstrated significantly higher beta-2 adrenergic receptor autoantibody titers compared to PCC patients and healthy controls. PCC patients showed a greater prevalence of borderline or pathological M3 muscarinic receptor autoantibodies. Higher beta-2 adrenergic autoantibody levels correlated with subjective autonomic symptoms in PCC and sympathovagal imbalance in ME/CFS. In ME/CFS patients, higher titers of M1, M3, and M4 muscarinic autoantibodies were unexpectedly associated with better verbal and working memory performance.

## Discussion

A key limitation is that ELISA measures autoantibody titers rather than functional activity, meaning it remains unconfirmed whether these antibodies block or stimulate the targeted receptors. The positive correlation between muscarinic autoantibodies and memory suggests a possible compensatory cholinergic mechanism that warrants further study. Additionally, the lack of complete overall separation between PCC and ME/CFS cohorts in Principal Component Analysis indicates highly overlapping, complex immunological profiles.

## Conclusion & Future Work

The researchers concluded that adrenergic and muscarinic GPCR autoantibodies are closely linked to autonomic and cognitive symptoms in both PCC and ME/CFS. These findings support further investigation of these autoantibodies as potential clinical biomarkers and therapeutic targets.
