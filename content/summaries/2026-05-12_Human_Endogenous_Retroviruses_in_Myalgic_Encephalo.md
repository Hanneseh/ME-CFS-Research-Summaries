## Summary

This comprehensive review consolidates a powerful mechanistic framework for ME/CFS, showing how environmental triggers can reactivate ancient viral DNA in our genome to sustain chronic illness. For patients and caregivers, this provides a clear, biological explanation for their symptoms, further dismantling the psychosomatic narrative. Furthermore, it points to practical clinical pathways: utilizing distinct "HERV fingerprints" as objective diagnostic blood tests, and repurposing existing therapies—such as HERV-targeting monoclonal antibodies like temelimab or immunotherapies like rituximab—to finally treat the root cause of the disease. This shifts ME/CFS closer to a stratified, treatable, and mechanism-driven condition.

## What was researched?

The review investigated the role of human endogenous retroviruses (HERVs) in the pathogenesis of Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (ME/CFS), exploring how their reactivated expression might link post-infectious immune dysfunction to chronic inflammation. It also evaluated the potential of HERVs as biomarkers for diagnosis and patient stratification, as well as targets for therapeutic interventions.

## Why was it researched?

ME/CFS lacks widely accepted, objective diagnostic biomarkers and definitive treatments, leaving its underlying etiology poorly understood. Because many cases are triggered by viral infections, researchers sought to explore whether the reactivation of ancient viral sequences embedded in our genome (HERVs) drives the persistent immune activation, epigenetic dysregulation, and metabolic/neuroendocrine dysfunction seen in patients.

## How was it researched?

The authors conducted a comprehensive review of emerging molecular, genetic, and clinical evidence surrounding HERV expression in ME/CFS and related autoimmune and neurological disorders. This involved analyzing findings from gene expression arrays (such as the HERV-V3 microarray), PCR studies, and histological analyses of patient tissues. They also mapped out the pathways of HERV-driven inflammation and assessed current and future therapeutic strategies.

## What has been found?

Evidence suggests that reactivated HERV subfamilies, particularly HERV-K and HERV-W, produce viral proteins and virus-like particles that act as persistent antigens, driving chronic immune activation and neuroinflammation. In tissues, duodenal biopsies from ME/CFS patients demonstrated elevated HERV-K protein expression co-localizing with plasmacytoid dendritic cells. Locus-specific profiling has revealed distinct "HERV fingerprints" capable of distinguishing ME/CFS from healthy controls, fibromyalgia, and Long COVID. Finally, the review highlights promising therapeutic options, including monoclonal antibodies like temelimab 💊, immunotherapies like rituximab 💊, and anti-retrovirals, which have demonstrated the ability to reduce HERV expression or alleviate symptoms in related conditions.

## Discussion

Although HERV signatures show immense promise, current research is limited by relatively small patient cohorts, heterogeneous diagnostic criteria, and inconsistent methodologies in older, global HERV profiling studies. Transitioning from identifying correlations to establishing direct causal links in ME/CFS pathology remains a major hurdle. However, advanced locus-specific profiling and machine-learning approaches represent powerful strategies to overcome these past limitations.

## Conclusion & Future Work

Reactivated HERVs represent a compelling pathological bridge between environmental triggers and chronic, multi-system dysfunction in ME/CFS. Future research must prioritize validating these HERV signatures in larger, multi-site cohorts using advanced viromics. Ultimately, this will pave the way for clinical trials stratified by HERV expression profiles to establish targeted, mechanism-driven therapies.
