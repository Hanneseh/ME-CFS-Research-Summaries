## Summary

This research highlights a significant decline in the prevalence of the most disabling symptom of Long COVID—post-exertional malaise (PEM)—as the pandemic progressed. Patients infected during the early pre-Omicron waves were over five times more likely to develop ME/CFS-like symptoms compared to those infected later. These findings suggest that changes in the virus, increased population immunity, or vaccination may be reducing the risk of developing the most severe, exhaustion-based forms of Long COVID.

## What was researched?

The study investigated the prevalence and determinants of post-exertional malaise (PEM) in patients with Post-COVID-19 Syndrome (PCS) across different phases of the pandemic.

## Why was it researched?

PEM is the hallmark symptom of ME/CFS and a major driver of disability in Long COVID, but it remains unclear why only a subset of patients experience it and whether the risk has changed over time.

## How was it researched?

Researchers conducted a retrospective review of 220 patients diagnosed with Post-COVID-19 Syndrome at a university hospital in France between 2020 and 2023. They compared clinical characteristics and infection dates between patients who experienced PEM and those who did not.

## What has been found?

PEM was identified in 26.4% of the participants and was strongly associated with higher overall fatigue levels. Patients infected during the 2020-2021 pre-Omicron period had a significantly higher risk of developing PEM compared to those infected in later waves. Specifically, an early infection date was linked to a five-fold increase in the likelihood of experiencing PEM.

## Discussion

The observed shift in PEM frequency may be due to differences in viral virulence between variants or the protective effects of accumulating immunity and vaccination. A limitation of the study is its retrospective design and the fact that it only included patients referred to a specialized hospital unit.

## Conclusion & Future Work

The frequency of PEM in Post-COVID-19 Syndrome has decreased across pandemic phases, indicating that early-wave patients may face a higher burden of ME/CFS-like symptoms. Clinical assessments for PCS should systematically include PEM screening to identify patients needing specialized energy management.
