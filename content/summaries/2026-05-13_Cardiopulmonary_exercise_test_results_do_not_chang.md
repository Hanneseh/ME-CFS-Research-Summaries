## Summary

This study provides high-quality clinical evidence that challenges the standard diagnostic and legal use of 2-day CPET drop-offs to prove disability or post-exertional malaise in ME/CFS. For patients and caregivers, this means that while a single CPET remains a useful measure of overall functional capacity, a failure to show a physiological decline on Day 2 does not disprove their diagnosis or disability. The research highlights the need to focus on tracking clinical symptom flares over the days following exertion rather than relying solely on real-time exercise performance to measure post-exertional malaise.

## What was researched?

This study investigated whether patients with ME/CFS demonstrate a decline in physiological parameters, such as peak oxygen consumption (VO2) and VO2 at the ventilatory threshold, during a second consecutive cardiopulmonary exercise test (CPET) performed 24 hours after the first. The researchers aimed to determine if these physiological metrics can reliably serve as objective markers for post-exertional malaise (PEM) or disability.

## Why was it researched?

A two-day CPET protocol is widely used in clinical and legal settings to document physical impairment and recovery deficits in ME/CFS. However, previous small-scale studies reported conflicting results regarding whether a significant day-to-day drop in physiological performance consistently occurs in these patients.

## How was it researched?

The researchers performed consecutive maximal bicycle ergometer CPETs on 58 patients with ME/CFS and 25 matched sedentary healthy controls. They measured respiratory gas exchange, heart rate, and subjective perceived exertion on both days. Peak VO2 and VO2 at the ventilatory threshold (VO2VT) were analyzed to assess functional capacity and recovery.

## What has been found?

The study found no significant changes in peak VO2 or VO2VT between Day 1 and Day 2 in either the ME/CFS or control groups. However, patients with ME/CFS demonstrated a significantly lower peak heart rate and reported a much higher perception of exertion (Borg scale) at all exercise levels compared to controls on both days. The objective physical performance of ME/CFS patients did not drop on the second day of testing.

## Discussion

These findings challenge the popular clinical assumption that a second-day drop in CPET metrics is a reliable objective biomarker for ME/CFS or disability. A key limitation is that the study did not track the multi-day symptom flare-ups typical of PEM following the exercise protocol. Additionally, because both groups maintained stable physiological parameters, the test itself may not capture the true neuromuscular or metabolic fatigue of the illness.

## Conclusion & Future Work

The authors concluded that 2-day CPET changes in Peak VO2 or VO2VT are not reliable markers for diagnosing ME/CFS, defining disability, or identifying PEM. Instead, they suggest that incorporating post-exercise subjective symptom monitoring would offer a more sensitive and specific measure of PEM.
