## Summary

This study introduces a novel imaging-based method to measure the interaction between immune cells and platelets, supporting the theory that Long COVID involves persistent thromboinflammation. By demonstrating significantly higher levels of cell aggregates in patients regardless of age, the research provides a potential objective biomarker for diagnosing the condition. This advancement could facilitate better patient stratification and more precise monitoring of treatments targeting vascular health.

## What was researched?

The study developed and validated an imaging flow cytometry method to quantify and characterize platelet-monocyte aggregates (PMA) in individuals with Long COVID.

## Why was it researched?

Researchers sought to define the role of thromboinflammatory mechanisms in Long COVID, as persistent systemic inflammation and endothelial dysfunction are central to the disease's pathology.

## How was it researched?

The researchers compared blood samples from 20 Long COVID patients and 20 healthy controls using a two-colour imaging flow cytometry assay. They specifically targeted CD14 for monocytes and CD62P for activated platelets to analyze the frequency and patterns of platelet attachment to individual immune cells.

## What has been found?

Circulating aggregates were significantly elevated in Long COVID patients at approximately 29.2% compared to only 4.6% in healthy controls. Furthermore, patients showed a distinct shift toward monocytes carrying multiple attached platelets, and these high levels were independent of age, unlike the age-related increase observed in the control group.

## Discussion

The imaging approach offers a distinct advantage over conventional methods by allowing researchers to visually confirm and categorize the complexity of cell-to-cell binding. This high-throughput technique confirms that platelets in Long COVID exist in a hyperactivated state, causing them to form clusters with immune cells even in the absence of acute infection.

## Conclusion & Future Work

Long COVID is associated with marked increases in platelet-monocyte aggregation, indicating sustained thromboinflammatory activity. These aggregates represent a sensitive cellular marker that may serve as a globally accessible biomarker for assessing thromboinflammatory burden.
