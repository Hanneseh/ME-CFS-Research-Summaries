## Summary

This study provides pilot-level biomarker association evidence highlighting a potential blood-testable signature for both ME/CFS and Long COVID with an ME/CFS phenotype. By showing that patients have higher levels of circulating plasma extracellular vesicles and altered mitochondrial potential within specific immune-derived vesicle subsets, the research supports the concept of a shared immune-metabolic disturbance. While these findings do not immediately change clinical practice, they provide a promising direction for developing objective diagnostic tools and tracking underlying disease mechanisms. It offers patients and caregivers validation by demonstrating tangible, measurable differences in blood-based immune and mitochondrial markers.

## What was researched?

This study investigated whether plasma-derived extracellular vesicles (EVs) exhibit shared molecular and metabolic signatures in patients with Long COVID (specifically those presenting with a myalgic encephalomyelitis/chronic fatigue syndrome phenotype, or LC-ME/CFS) and patients with classical, non-infected ME/CFS. Specifically, researchers profiled the surface markers of these vesicles to trace their cellular origins and measured their mitochondrial membrane potential.

## Why was it researched?

Both Long COVID and ME/CFS share significant clinical overlap, and both are hypothesized to involve underlying immune dysregulation and mitochondrial dysfunction. However, there is an urgent need for objective, blood-based biological markers to assist in diagnosis and to help distinguish these conditions from healthy or fully recovered states.

## How was it researched?

The researchers analyzed plasma extracellular vesicles from 125 individuals across two temporally distinct cohorts. The pandemic-era cohort (n = 75) included individuals who fully recovered from COVID-19, Long COVID patients with an ME/CFS phenotype (LC-ME/CFS), and pandemic-era ME/CFS patients without prior SARS-CoV-2 infection (pan-ME/CFS). The prepandemic cohort (n = 50) consisted of historical ME/CFS samples and age-matched healthy controls. Extracellular vesicles were isolated using size-exclusion chromatography, and their concentrations, sizes, cell-of-origin surface markers, and mitochondrial membrane potentials were characterized using nanoparticle tracking analysis and flow cytometry.

## What has been found?

Both the pan-ME/CFS and LC-ME/CFS groups exhibited significantly elevated plasma EV concentrations compared to COVID-recovered controls. Surface marker profiling showed that leukocyte-, monocyte/macrophage-, and platelet-derived EVs were increased, while B-cell-derived EVs were significantly reduced in both illness groups. Furthermore, the pan-ME/CFS group demonstrated an increase in mitochondrial membrane potential specifically within B-cell-, monocyte/macrophage-, and NK-cell-derived EV subsets. Prepandemic ME/CFS samples also replicated the finding of elevated mitochondrial membrane potential within the B-cell-derived EV subset.

## Discussion

A major limitation of the study is its cross-sectional design, which prevents drawing conclusions about whether these vesicle alterations cause the disease or are simply a byproduct of it. Additionally, different recruitment structures and an uneven sex distribution among the cohorts could affect the generalizability of the findings. Technical considerations also apply, as flow cytometry cannot definitively assign EV cellular origins, and the study did not measure total mitochondrial mass to normalize the membrane potential signal.

## Conclusion & Future Work

The authors concluded that ME/CFS and LC-ME/CFS patients share partially overlapping alterations in immune cell-derived extracellular vesicles and vesicular mitochondrial potential. These findings suggest that immune-metabolic signaling via extracellular vesicles is involved in the pathogenesis of both conditions. Future research should focus on larger longitudinal studies to validate these signatures as clinically useful biomarkers for patient stratification and diagnostic support.
