## Summary

This announcement represents a significant boost in research momentum and institutional recognition for ME/CFS and Long Covid. By using advanced "long-read" technology on thousands of patients, the project aims to go beyond superficial genetic associations to identify specific, rare structural genetic changes that could explain why people develop these diseases. For patients and caregivers, this funding brings hope that robust, high-quality genetic data will finally reveal the underlying biological mechanisms. Although this is not a clinical trial, it provides a crucial foundation for discovering concrete diagnostic tests and developing targeted therapeutic directions in the future.

## What was researched?

This announcement outlines the launch of the "Sequence ME & Long Covid" project, which has secured £4.75 million in UK government funding. The project aims to sequence the entire genomes of 9,000 people with ME/CFS and 9,000 people with Long Covid to identify rare and structural genetic variations that might drive these conditions.

## Why was it researched?

ME/CFS and Long Covid are severely debilitating illnesses affecting millions worldwide, yet they have suffered from decades of systemic underfunding. Researchers hope that discovering specific genetic variants and biological patterns will finally yield diagnostic biomarkers and pave the way for targeted treatments.

## How was it researched?

This project is an ambitious, large-scale genomic study built upon the previous DecodeME cohort. It utilizes Oxford Nanopore Technologies' cutting-edge "long-read" whole-genome sequencing to map all three billion letters of the genome. The initial phase utilizes the DNA samples of 6,000 ME/CFS participants already gathered via DecodeME, with plans to expand and recruit 9,000 Long Covid participants.

## What has been found?

As this is a funding and project launch announcement, there are no scientific results yet. However, the securing of £4.75 million from the UK's Office for Life Sciences (OLS), alongside funding from WE&ME, Solve M.E., and other partners, officially initiates Phase 2 of the study. This funding ensures the logistical setup and team recruitment necessary to generate the sequencing data.

## Discussion

The main strength of this study lies in its use of "long-read" sequencing, which is far superior to standard short-read technologies for detecting large-scale structural changes and complex gene combinations. A key limitation is that complete funding for the entire 18,000-person cohort is still being sought, although this latest grant secures the sequencing for the first 6,000 ME/CFS samples. Additionally, the project relies heavily on patient-partnership and co-production, keeping lived experience at the core.

## Conclusion & Future Work

The securing of substantial government funding marks a major milestone toward building the world's largest, comprehensive whole-genome sequencing dataset for post-viral syndromes. The researchers anticipate that these high-quality datasets will uncover precise biological mechanisms and disease subtypes. Ultimately, this work is expected to lay the foundation for future clinical trials and diagnostic tests.
