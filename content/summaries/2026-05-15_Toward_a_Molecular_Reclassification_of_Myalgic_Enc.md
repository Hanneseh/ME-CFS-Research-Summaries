## Summary

For patients and caregivers, this comprehensive review provides a powerful overview of the field's momentum, illustrating how modern science is shifting from treating ME/CFS as a vague symptom-based syndrome to an objectively measurable, mechanism-driven disease. It translates complex topics like genomics, T-cell exhaustion, and mitochondrial energy deficits into a cohesive, interconnected biological model. While these sophisticated machine learning platforms and molecular profiling methods are not yet standard clinic tools, they outline a clear pathway toward precision diagnostics and subgroup-specific therapies. This conceptual leap offers hope for moving beyond generic symptom management to targeted, biology-backed interventions.

## What was researched?

This narrative review synthesizes recent multi-omics and computational advances to outline a transition from symptom-based diagnosis of Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (ME/CFS) to a precise, molecularly defined classification. It specifically highlights the integration of machine learning and artificial intelligence with genomics, epigenetics, transcriptomics, immunology, and metabolomics.

## Why was it researched?

Historically, ME/CFS diagnosis has relied heavily on highly heterogeneous clinical symptoms and evolving case definitions due to a lack of validated, objective biomarkers. By summarizing recent breakthroughs, the researchers aim to provide a roadmap toward mechanistically defined subtypes, which could enable targeted diagnostic and therapeutic strategies.

## How was it researched?

The authors conducted a comprehensive narrative review of key literature surrounding molecular and computational progress in ME/CFS. They synthesized landmark studies including genome-wide association studies (GWAS) such as DecodeME, deep learning-powered genomic frameworks like HEAL2, epigenetic profiling, single-cell transcriptomics, and metabolomic pathway mapping. Furthermore, they evaluated advanced computational models—such as Positive Unlabeled Learning for cellular subsetting and the multi-modal deep learning platform BioMapAI—that integrate multi-layered physiological datasets.

## What has been found?

The review maps the progression of ME/CFS from genetic and epigenetic priming to systemic downstream consequences, including chronic innate immune activation and CD8+ T-cell exhaustion marked by TOX and EOMES transcription factors. Additionally, it outlines a cellular energy crisis driven by compromised mitochondrial coupling efficiency and system-wide hypometabolism. The authors highlight sex-specific transcriptomic and proteomic signatures that characterize maladaptive recovery. Finally, they underscore the power of AI tools to translate high-dimensional datasets into actionable precision models, pointing to circulating cell-free RNA signatures and the BioMapAI platform as key diagnostic and classification innovations.

## Discussion

The discussion emphasizes that while individual molecular markers may vary from patient to patient, they converge on the same core dysfunctional neuro-immune-metabolic pathways. This convergence suggests that molecular profiling could successfully resolve patient heterogeneity by stratifying individuals into distinct biological subtypes. However, a major challenge remains in translating these high-dimensional computational models into accessible clinical laboratory tests.

## Conclusion & Future Work

The authors conclude that integrating multi-omics with machine learning provides a robust foundation for a molecular reclassification of ME/CFS. Moving forward, validating these computational signatures in larger, independent longitudinal cohorts is essential to realize the promise of precision medicine. These advancements are expected to ultimately guide more targeted clinical trials and individualized treatments.
