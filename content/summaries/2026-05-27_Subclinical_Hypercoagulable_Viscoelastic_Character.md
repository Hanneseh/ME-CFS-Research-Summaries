## Summary

This study provides valuable biomarker association evidence for patients and caregivers by identifying a measurable, biological basis for post-exertional malaise (PEM) and fatigue after COVID-19. By demonstrating that individuals with these physical symptoms have firmer, fibrinogen-driven blood clots 15 months post-hospitalization, the research supports the 'microclot' and persistent thrombo-inflammation hypotheses of Long COVID. Clinically, it suggests that Rotational Thromboelastometry (ROTEM) could serve as a useful tool to identify patients who might benefit from therapies targeting clotting pathways. However, as an observational association study, it does not confirm causality or immediately justify the use of unprescribed blood thinners, which carry significant bleeding risks, but rather highlights a clear direction for future treatment trials.

## What was researched?

The study investigated whether survivors of COVID-19 acute lung injury or acute respiratory distress syndrome (ALI/ARDS) have persistent, subclinical evidence of hypercoagulability at 15 months after hospital discharge. Specifically, it looked at the association between blood clotting characteristics and physical symptoms of Post-Acute Sequelae of SARS-CoV-2 (PASC), such as fatigue and post-exertional malaise.

## Why was it researched?

Previous evidence suggests that chronic thrombo-inflammation and impaired fibrinolysis may play a central role in the development and persistence of Long COVID or PASC. Identifying measurable abnormalities in clotting could help explain the underlying biological mechanisms behind post-viral fatigue and help develop targeted therapeutic approaches.

## How was it researched?

Researchers conducted a single-center prospective cohort study of 93 adult survivors of COVID-19 ALI/ARDS who were hospitalized in 2020. At a 15-month follow-up, participants underwent Rotational Thromboelastometry (ROTEM) testing, a sensitive method used to assess clot formation dynamics and viscoelastic properties of whole blood. Participants also completed standardized questionnaires to assess the frequency and severity of symptoms using the NIH RECOVER definition, as well as specific phenotypes including fatigue, post-exertional malaise (PEM), and brain fog. Individuals taking dual antiplatelet therapy or anticoagulants, or those with genetic clotting disorders, were excluded to ensure accurate measurement.

## What has been found?

At the 15-month follow-up, 24% of the participants met the criteria for PASC, while 43% reported PEM, 27% reported fatigue, and 20% reported brain fog. Analysis revealed that an increased fibrinogen contribution to clot strength, measured as FIBTEM maximum clot firmness (MCF) on ROTEM, was directly and linearly associated with higher PASC risk. High FIBTEM MCF values exceeding normal reference ranges were significantly more common in those with PASC compared to those without (59% vs. 28%). These hypercoagulable clotting patterns were also significantly linked with post-exertional malaise (PEM) and fatigue, but not with brain fog.

## Discussion

A key strength of this study is the long-term (15-month) follow-up using ROTEM, which is highly sensitive to subtle, viscoelastic clotting dynamics that standard assays might miss. However, the study is limited by its single-center design, relatively small cohort of severe COVID-19 survivors, and lack of a non-hospitalized control group. Additionally, the lack of association with brain fog suggests that cognitive post-viral symptoms may be driven by distinct physiological mechanisms, such as neuroinflammation, rather than systemic hypercoagulability.

## Conclusion & Future Work

The researchers concluded that long-term physical symptoms of PASC, specifically fatigue and post-exertional malaise, are strongly linked to persistent subclinical hypercoagulability driven by fibrinogen dysregulation. This indicates that chronic clot-related pathology remains active long after severe acute respiratory infections. The authors suggest that further clinical research is required to evaluate targeted therapeutic approaches, such as antiplatelet or anticoagulant therapies, to alleviate these persistent physical symptoms.
