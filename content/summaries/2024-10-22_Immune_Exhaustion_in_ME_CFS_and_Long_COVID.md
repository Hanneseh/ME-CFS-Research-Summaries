## Summary

This pilot study provides exploratory biomarker evidence showing that while ME/CFS and long COVID share high levels of physical disability, their underlying immunological changes are mostly distinct. Rather than showing identical pathology, the data suggests ME/CFS presents more like a state of immune suppression, whereas long COVID displays persistent immune hyper-activation and exhaustion. For patients and caregivers, this research points to different underlying mechanisms that may eventually help doctors diagnose and tailor therapies specifically to each condition, though it does not immediately change current treatment protocols due to the small sample size.

## What was researched?

This study investigated altered gene expression profiles related to immune exhaustion in individuals with ME/CFS and long COVID concurrently. It aimed to explore similarities and differences in the immunological pathways of both patient cohorts compared to healthy controls.

## Why was it researched?

ME/CFS and long COVID share highly overlapping clinical presentations, such as severe fatigue and post-exertional malaise, but their exact biological mechanisms remain unclear. Investigating immune exhaustion, where prolonged cell stressors impair immune responses, can help identify objective biomarkers and potential therapeutic directions for these conditions.

## How was it researched?

The researchers isolated peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) from participants with ME/CFS (n = 14), long COVID (n = 15), and healthy controls (n = 18). They analyzed extracted RNA using the NanoString nCounter Immune Exhaustion gene expression panel to measure transcriptional differences. Participant clinical status was assessed via standardized criteria and the World Health Organization Disability Assessment Schedule (WHODAS).

## What has been found?

In ME/CFS, 5 genes were significantly upregulated and 9 genes were downregulated, pointing to lowered interferon signaling and immunoglobulin expression that suggests immune suppression. In long COVID, 15 genes were upregulated and 14 were downregulated, highlighting dysregulation in antigen presentation, cytokine signaling, and immune activation. Only 7 differentially expressed genes overlapped between both cohorts, indicating that while both experience severe disability, they follow distinct post-infectious immunopathologies.

## Discussion

A key strength of this study is the concurrent analysis of both cohorts under highly controlled conditions, but the small sample size limits the ability to stratify patients by symptom subtypes. While the results show distinct patterns of immune hyper-activation in long COVID versus immune suppression in ME/CFS, they represent exploratory pilot findings. Longitudinal research with larger cohorts is necessary to validate these transcriptional profiles.

## Conclusion & Future Work

The findings suggest that both ME/CFS and long COVID involve distinct adaptive and innate immune dysregulation. Future research should prioritize larger, stratified studies to map these immune exhaustion pathways for diagnostic and therapeutic development.
