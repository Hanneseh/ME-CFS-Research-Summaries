## Summary

This cross-sectional study provides critical clinical and biological clarity for patients and clinicians navigating Long COVID and post-vaccination symptoms. By demonstrating that post-acute COVID-19 vaccination syndrome (PACVS) causes similar levels of severe functional impairment to ME/CFS-phenotype Long COVID, the research validates the severity of post-vaccine injuries. Furthermore, the identification of elevated autoantibodies (anticardiolipin IgM and anti-U1-RNP) in PACVS patients offers concrete biological targets that could help with diagnostic testing and stratification. While the study does not suggest immediate treatment changes, it highlights autoimmune pathways as a vital focus for future targeted therapies.

## What was researched?

This study investigated the clinical and biological differences between post-acute sequelae of COVID-19 (PASC) with and without ME/CFS phenotypes, and post-acute COVID-19 vaccination syndrome (PACVS). The researchers sought to compare symptom prevalence, functional impairment, and immunologic biomarkers across these groups.

## Why was it researched?

Although PASC and PACVS share clinical features like chronic fatigue and cognitive impairment, they present distinct clinical and diagnostic challenges. Identifying specific symptom patterns and routine immunologic biomarkers is necessary to improve patient stratification and understand underlying disease mechanisms.

## How was it researched?

The researchers conducted a cross-sectional study of 181 patients from a post-COVID clinic at Columbia University Irving Medical Center. Participants were divided into three groups: PASC with ME/CFS (n = 71), PASC without ME/CFS (n = 82), and PACVS (n = 28). Symptoms, functional status, and perceived health levels were compared, and a sub-cohort of 89 patients received a complete immunologic workup, including cytokine panels and autoantibody assays (such as anticardiolipin IgM, anti-U1-RNP, and HSP-70).

## What has been found?

Fatigue was highly prevalent across all groups, with the ME/CFS group showing the highest overall multi-system symptom burden. PACVS patients reported significantly higher rates of atypical chief complaints, including peripheral neuropathy (17.9%), tinnitus (7.1%), and rash (10.7%). Functional impairment was comparably severe in both the ME/CFS and PACVS groups, with both reporting significantly greater disability than the non-ME/CFS Long COVID group. Immunologically, the PACVS group demonstrated significantly higher rates of anticardiolipin IgM (42.9%) and anti-U1-RNP (21.4%) positivity compared to non-ME/CFS Long COVID. High rates of HSP-70 autoantibody positivity and cytokine elevations were observed across all three groups.

## Discussion

The study's limitations include its single-center, cross-sectional design and reliance on self-reported symptoms, which may limit generalizability. The small sample size of the PACVS group (n = 28, with only 14 in the lab sub-cohort) also necessitates caution. However, a major strength is the rigorous classification of the ME/CFS phenotype and the inclusion of clinically accessible autoimmune biomarkers.

## Conclusion & Future Work

PASC and PACVS exhibit substantial clinical overlap but possess distinct immunologic signatures, particularly elevated autoantibodies in the PACVS cohort. These findings point to autoimmune mechanisms in post-vaccine complications and support the development of diagnostic panels to better identify and treat these overlapping conditions.
